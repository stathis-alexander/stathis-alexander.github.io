# Weeding Gardens

What does weeding gardens have to teach us about software engineering?

## Incremental Value

Each spring, the planter areas in front of my house spring up with weeds and rapidly become overrun. Each spring, I stare at the volume of weeds and hesitate to take care of it: where do I even start? The thing is, weeding, even for a short period, has _incremental value_. By just doing a little bit each day, and slowly burning down, I can easily and quickly outpace the growth and at the end of the week have a mostly weed-free garden.

Software engineering is the same. Big, ambiguous, or tangled projects can feel daunting and insurmountable first. The key is to figure out how to get started producing incremental value. It's a bit different than weeding; in weeding you know the end state. In software, you don't always. But if you can jump in and _get your hands dirty_ in software engineering, it often times has a similar result.

This doesn't mean just shipping to say you shipped. It means creating a list to burn down. It means picking an example and working it out fully. It means finding ways to deliver incremental value and learn as you go. Rather than being intimidated, start somewhere, with anything, and get working towards the end goal.

## Constant Maintenance

Weeding gardens is a never ending job. If you stop weeding your gardne, the weeds eventually come back. Gardens require constant maintenance, and so do codebases, systems, documentation. Building systems is not just about shipping them, but also about reinforcing them and tending to them regularly.
